## What Happened

### Micron's AI-memory thesis meets its trim line

Micron Technologies (MU), the largest American manufacturer of memory chips, rose 8.48% on Tuesday to a 52-week closing high of $487.48 — the largest single-day move in the Tier 1 cohort and an alpha of roughly 5.9 points over the semiconductor index. Three catalysts landed together: ASML raised its guide on AI-memory demand; SK Hynix's first-quarter print disclosed a 57% share of the high-bandwidth-memory market and a "wafer shortage until 2030"; and Anthropic disclosed $100bn-plus of committed compute with Amazon, alongside a separate Broadcom/Google TPU tranche scaling to three-plus gigawatts by 2027. Memory pricing has become the cleanest read on whether the AI buildout is pricing-disciplined or buying at any cost, and this week it is emphatically the latter. A lone counter-signal sits in the Form 4 file: Michael Cordano, Micron's executive vice-president of worldwide sales, sold 3,407 shares at $435 on 16 April in a discretionary open-market transaction. Micron now trades 2.6% below our $500 trim threshold — the upper end of sum-of-parts fair value in the deep-research file — and the next close above fires a 20–25% trim for existing positions. [CONFIRMED — ASML release, SK Hynix Q1, Anthropic/Amazon disclosures; SEC Form 4]

### Best Buy's chief executive steps down, and the tape reads a warning flare

Best Buy (BBY), America's largest consumer-electronics retailer, disclosed in an 8-K on Tuesday that Corie Barry, chief executive since 2019 and the first woman to lead the company, would step down on 31 October. She will be succeeded by Jason Bonfig, a twenty-five-year Best Buy veteran currently serving as senior executive vice-president of customer offering. Ms Barry's tenure spanned the pandemic's whipsaw — a surge in home-office demand followed by a hangover that has yet to clear — and the transition had not been flagged in the company's recent communications. Shares fell 4.6%, the single largest negative alpha in the Tier 1 cohort on Tuesday (-4.57 points against the consumer-discretionary index), and Goldman Sachs layered on a double-downgrade into the close, framing the succession as confirmation rather than cause of deterioration. Consumer electronics remain in a cyclical trough, and the tariff regime tightening around Chinese-made goods hits Best Buy harder than most peers; the next watched price level is the $55 starter threshold, 13% below Tuesday's close. Mr Bonfig's first investor communication will be the thesis-defining input from here. [CONFIRMED — Best Buy IR; SEC 8-K Accession 0000764478-26-000012]

### America's defence primes de-rate despite clean beats

Northrop Grumman (NOC) fell 3.52% and Raytheon Technologies (RTX) fell 3.34% on Tuesday, both on clean first-quarter prints. Northrop reported revenue of $9.88bn (up 4.4%), GAAP earnings of $6.14 per share (up 85%), and reaffirmed a full-year guide of $43.5–44bn; Raytheon printed revenue of $22.08bn (up 8.7%), free cash flow of $1.31bn (up 65%), a record $271bn backlog, a book-to-bill of 1.56, and raised its full-year guide to $92.5–93.5bn and $6.70–6.90 of adjusted earnings per share. Lockheed Martin (LMT) fell 2.89% into Wednesday's earnings release, Huntington Ingalls (HII) fell 3.00%, and the iShares Aerospace & Defense ETF (ITA) dropped 1.77%; a third consecutive down session across all five primes. A tanker reportedly detained in the Strait of Hormuz overnight has not, so far, reversed the drift, which is itself a signal about how the market is pricing Middle-East risk today. The most plausible read is a post-election budget-discipline overlay combined with profit-taking after a strong first quarter; the alternative — that backlog-to-revenue conversion is being priced down — would be more serious. Raytheon has been moved to the deep-research queue; the tell will be whether Lockheed Martin's Wednesday-morning print breaks the pattern or confirms it. [CONFIRMED — NOC and RTX Q1 releases; REPORTED — Reuters on Hormuz detention]

### Hormuz oil bid and tariff mechanism advance

West Texas Intermediate crude rose 2.46% to $94.40 and Brent rose 1.35% to $103.29 on Tuesday after Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps seized two merchant vessels — the MSC Francesca and the Epaminondas — in the Strait of Hormuz, the first such action since the ceasefire extension. Product moves were asymmetric: gasoline fell 2.50% and heating oil fell 2.78%, a pattern consistent with supply-risk pricing rather than demand strength. Trade-press reports, which we treat as unverified, suggest a physical-to-futures gap of roughly $150 against $101 — a gap whose upward convergence would imply more downside for fuel-exposed cruise and airline names than markets currently price. Separately, US Customs and Border Protection launched Phase 1 of the IEEPA tariff-refund mechanism on Monday, covering roughly $166bn of collected duties; reports suggest the Commerce Department is moving to impose tariffs of up to 3,521% on Cambodian, Malaysian, Thai, and Vietnamese goods via Sections 232 and 301, which we are tagging REPORTED pending primary-source verification. [CONFIRMED — Hormuz seizures multi-wire, CBP 20 April announcement; REPORTED — physical-futures gap, Southeast Asia tariff scope]
